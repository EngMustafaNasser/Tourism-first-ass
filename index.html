The pyramids

The Pyramids of Cairo, also known as the Pyramids of Giza, are ancient structures built during the Old Kingdom period of ancient Egypt, around 2580–2560 BC. Built as tombs for the pharaohs, the Great Pyramid of Giza was the largest, standing about 146 metres (490 ft) tall. These pyramids, which are precisely aligned with the cardinal points of the compass, are major tourist attractions today, attracting millions of visitors each year. Next to the pyramids stands the Great Sphinx of Giza, another symbol of ancient Egypt. Despite their age, efforts are ongoing to preserve and protect these magnificent structures.

 Al-Azhar Mosque

For fans of Islamic architecture, Al-Azhar Mosque is a must-see. Founded by the Fatimid Jawhar al-Siqilli in 970, it is now a stunning blend of different styles and periods. For example, the qibla wall dates back to the 14th century, Sultan Qaytbay added a minaret in 1475, the main façade dates back to the 19th century, and the double-arched portal dates back to the mid-18th century. Visitors can enjoy the intermingling of Fatimid, Mamluk and Ottoman architectural features in this magnificent building. It should be noted that the mosque and school complex at Al-Azhar is one of the oldest universities in the world. Al-Azhar is distinguished by its unique architectural style that reflects the history of Islamic art, and part of its uniqueness lies in the fact that it was a center for Shiite theological studies until the 1260s, before reopening as a Sunni institution. The mosque is named after Fatima al-Zahra, the daughter of the Prophet Muhammad, and is a beacon for Islamic scholars around the world.
Cairo Citadel or Saladin Citadel

A massive citadel in Islamic Cairo, the Citadel of Sultan Saladin al-Ayyubi stands tall atop the Mokattam Hills. Originally built by Saladin in 1176 AD, it became the official residence of Egypt’s rulers under Sultan al-Kamil ibn al-Adil in 1207 AD. Despite its historical significance, the Citadel’s role as a seat of power shifted to Abdeen Palace in the 19th century. Witnessing pivotal moments in Egyptian history, it now offers visitors a wealth of attractions, including the famous Mosque of Muhammad Ali Pasha and other notable sites such as the Mosque of al-Nasir Muhammad ibn Qalawun and the Mosque of Sulayman Pasha al-Khadem. It also houses several museums, including the Police Museum, the Jewel Palace Museum, and the Military Museum.

 Baron Empain Palace

Baron Empain Palace, with its distinctive Indian design and prominent tower in the desert of Heliopolis, is a prominent landmark in the capital. It was built between 1907 and 1911 by Edouard Louis Joseph Empain, the famous Belgian baron, and designed by the French architect Alexandre Marcel. The palace consists of a basement and two floors, with a roof, and is surrounded by a large garden. The basement includes servants’ quarters, while a spiral staircase and an elevator connect the floors. The ground floor contains reception, dining and billiard rooms, while the first floor contains four rooms with balconies and bathrooms. The roof of the palace is distinguished by its wonderful views and is considered part of the palace’s distinctive tower.
Grand Egyptian Museum

The Egyptian Museum is the oldest archaeological museum in the Middle East, and houses the largest collection of ancient Egyptian antiquities in the world, spanning from the Predynastic Period to the Greco-Roman era. The building was designed by French architect Marcel Dourgnon after winning an international competition in 1895. The museum was opened in 1902 by Khedive Abbas Hilmi II, and has become a historic landmark in downtown Cairo. The museum features a large collection of rare artifacts, including the funerary collection of Yuya and Thuya, statues of great pharaohs such as Khufu, Khafre and Menkaure, as well as papyri, coffins and jewelry that complete the museum’s collection.

 National Museum of Egyptian Civilization

The National Museum of Egyptian Civilization is the first museum of its kind in Egypt and the Arab world, and is an integrated global cultural complex that allows its visitors the opportunity to explore successive Egyptian civilizations. The idea of ​​establishing it began in 1982 at the request of the Egyptian government, and the foundation stone was laid in 2002. Some halls were opened in 2017, and it was officially opened in 2021 and 2022. It includes a variety of artifacts that represent Egypt's heritage throughout different eras, from ancient times to the modern era. The museum also includes halls for mummies and Egyptian textiles, and hosts the Egyptian Civilization School to increase cultural and archaeological awareness among citizens and foreign visitors.
